Why utility? (Berger $$2.1)

Two problems with “value”

In evaluating consequences of actions, two problems arise:

1. Some consequences have no natural scale. Prestige, customer goodwill, reputation – important to businesses, but hard to quantify. A company considering selling its name-brand product in discount stores can estimate the immediate profit, but the long-term effect on prestige is much harder to measure.

2. Even when a monetary scale exists, it may not reflect true value. Suppose you can do an unpleasant task for $100. At your current income, you might do it. After receiving a million dollars, you probably would not. The value of $100 depends on context.

Value is not linear in money

Suppose you are offered a choice:

\(\quad\) (A) Receive $200,000 for certain.

\(\quad\) (B) A 50–50 chance of receiving $0 or $410,000.

The expected monetary value of (B) is $205,000 \(>\) $200,000. Yet most people prefer (A).

This means the “value” of money is not linear in the amount. The marginal value of money is decreasing: the difference in value between $0 and $200,000 is larger than the difference between $200,000 and $410,000.

We need a way to assign numerical values to consequences that reflects the decision maker’s true preferences. That is what utility theory does.

Utility theory (Berger $$2.2)

The setup

Let \(\mathcal{R}\) be the set of all possible consequences (rewards). Let \(\mathcal{P}\) be the set of all probability distributions on \(\mathcal{R}\).

We assume the decision maker can state preferences among elements of \(\mathcal{P}\):

  • \(P_1 \prec P_2\): \(P_2\) is preferred to \(P_1\).
  • \(P_1 \approx P_2\): \(P_1\) and \(P_2\) are equivalent.
  • \(P_1 \preceq P_2\): \(P_1\) is not preferred to \(P_2\).

A reward \(r \in \mathcal{R}\) is identified with the degenerate distribution \((r)\) that puts probability one on \(r\). So preferences apply to rewards as well.

The goal: find a function \(U: \mathcal{R}\to \mathbb{R}\) such that \(P_1 \prec P_2 \iff E^{P_1}[U(r)] < E^{P_2}[U(r)]\). Such a \(U\) is called a utility function.

Constructing a utility function

Assume a utility function exists. Here is one way to build it.

Step 1. Choose two rewards \(r_1 \prec r_2\). Set \(U(r_1) = 0\), \(U(r_2) = 1\). (This fixes the scale.)

Step 2. For any \(r_3\) with \(r_1 \prec r_3 \prec r_2\): find \(\alpha \in (0,1)\) such that \(r_3 \approx \alpha(r_1) + (1-\alpha)(r_2)\). Then \(U(r_3) = 1 - \alpha\).

The idea: \(r_3\) is equivalent to a gamble between \(r_1\) (prob. \(\alpha\)) and \(r_2\) (prob. \(1-\alpha\)). Then \(U(r_3) = \alpha U(r_1) + (1-\alpha)U(r_2) = 1-\alpha\).

Step 3. For \(r_3 \prec r_1\): find \(\alpha\) such that \(r_1 \approx \alpha(r_3) + (1-\alpha)(r_2)\). Then \(0 = \alpha U(r_3) + (1-\alpha)\), giving \(U(r_3) = -(1-\alpha)/\alpha < 0\).

Step 4. For \(r_2 \prec r_3\): find \(\alpha\) such that \(r_2 \approx \alpha(r_1) + (1-\alpha)(r_3)\). Then \(1 = (1-\alpha)U(r_3)\), giving \(U(r_3) = 1/(1-\alpha) > 1\).

Step 5 (Consistency). If \(U(r_3), U(r_4), U(r_5)\) are found with \(r_3 \prec r_4 \prec r_5\), find \(\alpha\) with \(r_4 \approx \alpha(r_3) + (1-\alpha)(r_5)\). Check: \(U(r_4) = \alpha U(r_3) + (1-\alpha)U(r_5)\).

Example: football or movie? (Berger, Ex. 1) – setup

Saturday afternoon: \(a_1\) = football game, \(a_2\) = movie. There is a 40% chance of rain (\(\theta_1\)).

Four possible outcomes (action, weather):

\(r_1 = (a_1, \theta_1)\): football, rain (ruined afternoon – worst)
\(r_2 = (a_1, \theta_2)\): football, no rain (great day – best)
\(r_3 = (a_2, \theta_1)\): movie, rain (good movie, smart choice)
\(r_4 = (a_2, \theta_2)\): movie, no rain (okay, slight regret)

Preference ordering: \(r_1 \prec r_4 \prec r_3 \prec r_2\).

Fix the scale: \(U(r_1) = 0\), \(U(r_2) = 1\). We need to determine \(U(r_3)\) and \(U(r_4)\).

Determining the utilities: two subjective judgments

To find \(U(r_4)\) and \(U(r_3)\), the decision maker asks: “at what gamble between \(r_1\) (worst) and \(r_2\) (best) am I indifferent to this reward?”

Judgment 1: “I am indifferent between \(r_4\) (movie, no rain) and a gamble that gives \(r_1\) with probability 0.4 and \(r_2\) with probability 0.6.” That is: \(r_4 \approx 0.4(r_1) + 0.6(r_2)\).

This gives \(U(r_4) = 0.4 \cdot 0 + 0.6 \cdot 1 = 0.6\).

Judgment 2: “I am indifferent between \(r_3\) (movie, rain) and a gamble that gives \(r_1\) with probability 0.3 and \(r_2\) with probability 0.7.” That is: \(r_3 \approx 0.3(r_1) + 0.7(r_2)\).

This gives \(U(r_3) = 0.3 \cdot 0 + 0.7 \cdot 1 = 0.7\).

Both are subjective: they come from introspection. Different people could reasonably give different numbers.

The consistency check: a third judgment that is not free

We now have \(U(r_1) = 0\), \(U(r_4) = 0.6\), \(U(r_3) = 0.7\), \(U(r_2) = 1\).

Judgment 3: The decision maker compares \(r_3\) to a gamble between \(r_4\) and \(r_2\). “At what \(\alpha\) am I indifferent between \(r_3\) and \(\alpha(r_4) + (1-\alpha)(r_2)\)?” After reflection: \(\alpha = 0.6\).

But if the utility function from Judgments 1 and 2 is correct: \[ U(r_3) = \alpha\, U(r_4) + (1-\alpha)\, U(r_2) \quad \implies \quad 0.7 = 0.6\alpha + (1-\alpha) \quad \implies \quad \alpha = 0.75. \]

The decision maker said \(\alpha = 0.6\), but the first two judgments force \(\alpha = 0.75\). At least one of the three judgments is wrong.

This is the point: once two utilities are fixed, the third comparison is determined. If it disagrees, the decision maker must revise. Suppose the revised values are \(U(r_4) = 0.6\) and \(U(r_3) = 0.75\).

Making the decision

With the consistent utilities \(U(r_1) = 0\), \(U(r_3) = 0.75\), \(U(r_4) = 0.6\), \(U(r_2) = 1\):

Expected utility of each action: \[ \begin{aligned} a_1 \text{ (football)}: \quad &\pi(\theta_1)\, U(r_1) + \pi(\theta_2)\, U(r_2) = (0.4)(0) + (0.6)(1) = 0.6. \\ a_2 \text{ (movie)}: \quad &\pi(\theta_1)\, U(r_3) + \pi(\theta_2)\, U(r_4) = (0.4)(0.75) + (0.6)(0.6) = 0.66. \end{aligned} \]

The optimal action: go to the movie.

The raw payoffs might have suggested football (higher upside). But the utility function encodes an asymmetry: a ruined afternoon (\(U = 0\)) is much worse than the mild regret of missing the game when it did not rain (\(U = 0.6\), not that bad). That asymmetry tips the balance.

The axioms

For the construction to work and for \(U\) to represent preferences, the preference ordering must be “rational.” Four axioms:

Axiom 1 (Completeness): For any \(P_1, P_2 \in \mathcal{P}\), either \(P_1 \prec P_2\), \(P_1 \approx P_2\), or \(P_2 \prec P_1\).

Axiom 2 (Transitivity): If \(P_1 \preceq P_2\) and \(P_2 \preceq P_3\), then \(P_1 \preceq P_3\).

Axiom 3 (Independence): If \(P_1 \prec P_2\), then \(\alpha P_1 + (1-\alpha)P_3 \prec \alpha P_2 + (1-\alpha)P_3\) for any \(0 < \alpha < 1\) and \(P_3 \in \mathcal{P}\).

Axiom 4 (Continuity): If \(P_1 \prec P_2 \prec P_3\), there exist \(0 < \alpha, \beta < 1\) such that \(\alpha P_1 + (1-\alpha) P_3 \prec P_2\) and \(P_2 \prec \beta P_1 + (1-\beta) P_3\).

Interpreting the axioms

Axioms 1 and 2 are housekeeping: you can compare anything, and your comparisons are transitive.

Axiom 3 (independence): if you prefer \(P_2\) to \(P_1\), mixing both with the same \(P_3\) does not reverse the preference. Replacing \(P_1\) by \(P_2\) in one component of a mixture is always an improvement.

Axiom 4 (continuity): no consequence is infinitely good or infinitely bad. If \(P_1\) were infinitely bad (say, death), there would be no \(\beta > 0\) for which you would risk it to get \(P_3\) instead of \(P_2\). But if death were truly infinitely bad, you would never cross a street, never drive a car. People do these things, so Axiom 4 holds in practice.

The existence theorem and uniqueness

Theorem. If the preference ordering on \(\mathcal{P}\) satisfies Axioms 1–4, there exists a utility function \(U: \mathcal{R}\to \mathbb{R}\) such that \(P_1 \prec P_2 \iff E^{P_1}[U(r)] < E^{P_2}[U(r)]\).

We will not prove existence. The proof constructs \(U\) using the procedure from earlier and verifies it represents the preferences; see DeGroot (1970). What we will prove are two properties of \(U\) that we will use.

Uniqueness up to affine transformation. If \(U\) is a utility function, so is \(V = bU + c\) for any \(b > 0\). Proof: \[ \begin{aligned} E^{P_1}[U(r)] < E^{P_2}[U(r)] &\iff b\,E^{P_1}[U(r)] + c < b\,E^{P_2}[U(r)] + c \\ &\iff E^{P_1}[V(r)] < E^{P_2}[V(r)]. \end{aligned} \] The preferences are unchanged. \(\square\)

This means \(U(r_1) = 0\), \(U(r_2) = 1\) is just a convention. Any two reference points give an equivalent utility function.

Axiom 4 implies boundedness

Proposition. If the preference ordering satisfies Axioms 1–4 and \(U\) is the resulting utility function, then \(U\) is bounded.

Proof. Suppose for contradiction that \(U\) is unbounded above: there exist rewards \(s_1, s_2, \ldots \in \mathcal{R}\) with \(U(s_n) \to \infty\).

Choose weights \(p_n > 0\) with \(\sum p_n = 1\) and \(\sum p_n U(s_n) = \infty\). Define \(Q = \sum p_n (s_n) \in \mathcal{P}\).

Pick any two rewards \(a \prec b\). Since \(E^Q[U(r)] = \infty > U(b) > U(a)\): \((a) \prec (b) \prec Q\).

Axiom 4 says: there exists \(\alpha \in (0,1)\) such that \(\alpha(a) + (1-\alpha)Q \prec (b)\), i.e., \(E^{\alpha(a) + (1-\alpha)Q}[U(r)] < U(b)\).

But for any \(\alpha \in (0,1)\): \[ E^{\alpha(a)+(1-\alpha)Q}[U(r)] = \alpha\, U(a) + (1-\alpha)\cdot \infty = \infty > U(b). \]

No such \(\alpha\) exists. Contradiction. Symmetric argument for unbounded below. \(\square\)

Properties of monetary utility functions

When \(\mathcal{R}\) is monetary, \(U(r)\) typically has four features:

(i) Linear for small \(r\). You would take a fair coin flip on \(\pm 25\) cents. So \(U\) is linear near \(r = 0\). The “small” range depends on context: 25 cents for a student, millions for a large corporation.

(ii) Concave for \(r > 0\). Marginal utility \(U'(r)\) is decreasing, so \(U''(r) < 0\). An additional $100 matters more at $0 than at $1,000,000.

(iii) Different shape for gains and losses. People are often risk-seeking for losses (willing to gamble to avoid a sure loss) but risk-averse for gains. So \(U(r)\) may be convex for \(r < 0\) and concave for \(r > 0\).

(iv) Bounded. Few would assign meaningful additional utility to money beyond $10\(^{100}\).

From utility to loss (Berger $$2.4.1)

Defining the loss function

Each pair \((\theta, a)\) determines a consequence with utility \(U(\theta, a)\). The loss function is \[ L(\theta, a) = -U(\theta, a). \]

Maximizing expected utility is the same as minimizing expected loss.

This is the answer to our second question: why is expected loss the right criterion? Because the utility axioms tell us that expected utility is the correct measure of value under uncertainty. Expected loss inherits this justification.

The Bayesian expected loss \(\rho(\pi, a) = E_\pi[L(\theta, a)]\), the risk \(R(\theta, \delta) = E_{P_\theta}[L(\theta, \delta(X))]\), the Bayes risk \(r(\pi, \delta)\), and the loss of a randomized rule – all are justified by this construction.

When consequences are random

Often \((\theta, a)\) does not directly determine the consequence. The reward is a random variable \(Z\) whose distribution depends on \(\theta\) and \(a\). Then: \[ U(\theta, a) = E_{\theta, a}[U(Z)], \qquad L(\theta, a) = -E_{\theta, a}[U(Z)]. \]

One must be careful to define \(Z\) as the overall outcome.

Properties of loss functions

1. Invariance under positive affine transformations.

Claim: If \(L\) is a valid loss, then \(L' = bL + c\) (with \(b > 0\)) leads to the same optimal decisions.

Proof (for the Bayes case): The Bayes risk is \(r(\pi, \delta) = E_\pi[R(\theta, \delta)]\). Under \(L'\): \[ r'(\pi, \delta) = E_\pi[E_{P_\theta}[bL(\theta, \delta(X)) + c]] = b\, r(\pi, \delta) + c. \] Since \(b > 0\), the \(\delta\) minimizing \(r'\) is the same as the \(\delta\) minimizing \(r\). \(\square\)

So we can always shift: \(L^*(\theta, a) = L(\theta, a) - \inf_{\theta, a} L(\theta, a) \geq 0\).

2. Regret loss. \(\ell(\theta, a) = \sup_{a'} U(\theta, a') - U(\theta, a)\): the loss relative to the best action for the true \(\theta\). Equivalent to \(L\) for Bayesian analysis, but can differ for minimax. We return to this in Chapter 5.

Standard loss functions (Berger $$2.4.2)

I. Squared-error loss

\[ L(\theta, a) = (\theta - a)^2. \]

Why is it popular?

  • For unbiased estimators, \(R(\theta, \delta) = \mathrm{Var}_{P_\theta}(\delta(X))\).
  • Related to least squares theory.
  • Calculations are often tractable.

Berger’s honest assessment: “The above justifications really have very little merit. The question is – does squared-error loss typically reflect the true loss?”

Squared-error loss is unbounded and convex: large errors are penalized very severely. In many real problems, the loss should be bounded and (for large errors) concave.

Example: risk of the sample mean under squared-error loss

\(X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\theta, \sigma^2)\). Estimate \(\theta\) with \(\delta(X) = \bar{X}\).

\[ R(\theta, \delta) = E_{P_\theta}[(\theta - \bar{X})^2] = \mathrm{Var}_{P_\theta}(\bar{X}) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}. \]

The risk is constant in \(\theta\) and decreases in \(n\).

Now consider \(\delta_c(X) = c\bar{X}\) for some \(c \in \mathbb{R}\). Then: \[ R(\theta, \delta_c) = E_{P_\theta}[(\theta - c\bar{X})^2] = c^2 \frac{\sigma^2}{n} + (1-c)^2\theta^2. \]

This is Example 4 from Lecture 1 with \(\sigma^2/n\) replacing 1. Minimizing over \(c\) for a fixed \(\theta\) gives \(c^* = \theta^2/(\theta^2 + \sigma^2/n)\), which depends on \(\theta\) – so the “optimal shrinkage” is unknowable. This tension is at the heart of the course.

The Taylor expansion defense of squared-error loss

Suppose the reward is \(Z = h(\theta - a, Y)\), where \(Y\) is an extraneous random variable independent of \(\theta\) and \(a\).

Define \(g(\theta - a, Y) = U(h(\theta - a, Y))\). If the sample information is accurate, \(\theta - a\) is small. Expand in Taylor series around 0: \[ g(\theta - a, Y) \approx g(0, Y) + (\theta - a)g'(0, Y) + \tfrac{1}{2}(\theta - a)^2 g''(0, Y). \]

Taking expectations over \(Y\), with \(K_1 = -E_Y[g(0,Y)]\), \(K_2 = -E_Y[g'(0,Y)]\), \(K_3 = -\frac{1}{2}E_Y[g''(0,Y)]\): \[ L(\theta, a) \approx K_1 + K_2(\theta - a) + K_3(\theta - a)^2. \]

Completing the square (assuming \(K_3 > 0\)): \[ L(\theta, a) \approx K_3\!\left(\theta - a + \frac{K_2}{2K_3}\right)^{\!2} + \text{const}. \]

When \(K_2 = 0\) (symmetric utility, symmetric \(Y\)), this is squared-error loss.

Variants of squared-error loss

Weighted squared-error: \(L(\theta, a) = w(\theta)(\theta - a)^2\).

The weight \(w(\theta) > 0\) encodes that a given error may matter more at some parameter values than others.

Example: estimating a Poisson rate \(\theta > 0\). An error of 1 when \(\theta = 0.5\) is much worse than the same error when \(\theta = 100\). The weight \(w(\theta) = 1/\theta^2\) normalizes for scale, giving \(L(\theta, a) = (1 - a/\theta)^2\).

Quadratic loss (multivariate): \(\theta, a \in \mathbb{R}^p\), \(Q\) positive definite: \[ L(\theta, a) = (\theta - a)^T Q (\theta - a). \] When \(Q = I_p\): \(L(\theta, a) = \|\theta - a\|^2 = \sum_{i=1}^p (\theta_i - a_i)^2\).

We will encounter this extensively when studying Stein estimation in Week 6.

Example: risk under sum-of-squares loss

\(X = (X_1, \ldots, X_p)^T\) with \(X_i \overset{\text{ind}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\theta_i, 1)\). Estimate \(\theta = (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_p)^T\) under \(L(\theta, a) = \|\theta - a\|^2\).

The natural estimator is \(\delta(X) = X\). Its risk: \[ R(\theta, \delta) = E_{P_\theta}\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^p (\theta_i - X_i)^2\right] = \sum_{i=1}^p \mathrm{Var}_{P_\theta}(X_i) = p. \]

Constant risk \(p\), for all \(\theta\). Seems hard to improve.

For \(p \geq 3\), James and Stein (1960) showed the estimator \[ \delta^{JS}(X) = \left(1 - \frac{p-2}{\|X\|^2}\right)X \] satisfies \(R(\theta, \delta^{JS}) < p\) for all \(\theta\). The sample mean vector is inadmissible.

That result is one of the major topics later in the course.

II. Linear loss

When the utility function is approximately linear over the relevant range, the loss is approximately linear: \[ L(\theta, a) = \begin{cases} K_0(\theta - a) & \text{if } \theta - a \geq 0 \quad \text{(underestimation)} \\ K_1(a - \theta) & \text{if } \theta - a < 0 \quad \text{(overestimation)} \end{cases} \]

\(K_0\) and \(K_1\) encode the relative cost of under- vs. overestimation.

When \(K_0 = K_1\): absolute error loss \(L(\theta, a) = |\theta - a|\).

Recall Lecture 1, Example 1 (drug company): \(L(\theta_2, a) = (\theta_2 - a)\) if \(\theta_2 \geq a\) and \(2(a - \theta_2)\) if \(\theta_2 < a\). That is linear loss with \(K_0 = 1\), \(K_1 = 2\): overestimating the adverse reaction rate costs twice as much as underestimating.

Example: car production under linear loss (Berger, Ex. 17)

A company must decide how many cars \(a\) to produce. The demand is \(\theta\) (unknown). Each car sold yields $500 profit. Each unsold car yields $1000 loss.

If \(a \leq \theta\): all cars sell, profit = \(500a\). If \(a > \theta\): \(\theta\) cars sell, \(a - \theta\) do not, profit = \(500\theta - 1000(a - \theta) = 1500\theta - 1000a\).

The regret loss: the best action is \(a^* = \theta\), profit \(500\theta\). So: \[ \ell(\theta, a) = 500\theta - \text{profit}(a) = \begin{cases} 500(\theta - a) & \text{if } a \leq \theta \\ 1500(a - \theta) & \text{if } a > \theta \end{cases} \]

Linear loss with \(K_0 = 500\) (opportunity cost of underproduction) and \(K_1 = 1500\) (cost of overproduction). The asymmetry \(K_1/K_0 = 3\) reflects that unsold cars are much worse than unmet demand.

III. “0–1” loss

In a two-action problem (testing \(H_0: \theta \in \Theta_0\) vs. \(H_1: \theta \in \Theta_1\)): \[ L(\theta, a_i) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } \theta \in \Theta_i \quad \text{(correct)} \\ 1 & \text{if } \theta \in \Theta_j,\ j \neq i \quad \text{(incorrect)} \end{cases} \]

The risk function:

  • When \(\theta \in \Theta_0\): \(R(\theta, \delta) = P_{P_\theta}(\text{reject } H_0)\) = Type I error.
  • When \(\theta \in \Theta_1\): \(R(\theta, \delta) = P_{P_\theta}(\text{accept } H_0)\) = Type II error.

The Bayesian expected loss: \(\rho(\pi^*, a_i) = 1 - \pi^*(\theta \in \Theta_i)\).

The error probabilities from Neyman–Pearson theory are the risk function under 0–1 loss. Testing is a special case of decision theory.

Example: 0–1 loss for a normal test

\(X \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta, 1)\). Test \(H_0: \theta \leq 0\) vs. \(H_1: \theta > 0\). Decision rule: reject \(H_0\) if \(X > c\).

Under 0–1 loss, the risk function is: \[ R(\theta, \delta_c) = \begin{cases} P_{P_\theta}(X > c) = 1 - \Phi(c - \theta) & \text{if } \theta \leq 0 \quad \text{(Type I)} \\ P_{P_\theta}(X \leq c) = \Phi(c - \theta) & \text{if } \theta > 0 \quad \text{(Type II)} \end{cases} \]

For \(c = 0\): Type I error at \(\theta = 0\) is \(1 - \Phi(0) = 0.5\). Type II error at \(\theta = 1\) is \(\Phi(-1) = 0.159\).

For \(c = 1.645\): Type I error at \(\theta = 0\) is \(1 - \Phi(1.645) = 0.05\). Type II error at \(\theta = 1\) is \(\Phi(0.645) = 0.74\).

The familiar tradeoff: making \(R(\theta, \delta_c)\) smaller for \(\theta \leq 0\) makes it larger for \(\theta > 0\), and vice versa.

Weighted 0–1 loss

Basic 0–1 loss treats the two types of error as equally costly. Rarely appropriate.

More realistic: \[ L(\theta, a_i) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if correct} \\ K_0 & \text{if reject } H_0 \text{ when } \theta \in \Theta_0 \quad \text{(Type I)} \\ K_1 & \text{if accept } H_0 \text{ when } \theta \in \Theta_1 \quad \text{(Type II)} \end{cases} \]

The risk becomes \(K_0 \cdot P_{P_\theta}(\text{reject } H_0)\) for \(\theta \in \Theta_0\) and \(K_1 \cdot P_{P_\theta}(\text{accept } H_0)\) for \(\theta \in \Theta_1\).

Even more generally: \(L(\theta, a_1) = K_0(\theta)\) for \(\theta \in \Theta_0\), where the weight varies within \(\Theta_0\). A Type I error may be worse when \(\theta\) is far from the boundary.

Loss for inference problems (Berger $$2.4.3)

Inference as a decision problem

The most common use: representing standard inference measures as a risk or Bayesian expected loss.

Confidence sets. Let \(C(x) \subseteq \Theta\) be a confidence rule. The loss \[ L(\theta, C(x)) = 1 - I_{C(x)}(\theta) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } \theta \in C(x) \\ 1 & \text{if } \theta \notin C(x) \end{cases} \] gives \(R(\theta, C) = 1 - P_{P_\theta}(C(X) \ni \theta)\): one minus the coverage probability.

Unbiased estimation. Restrict to \(E_{P_\theta}[\delta(X)] = \theta\) for all \(\theta\). Under squared-error loss, \(R(\theta, \delta) = \mathrm{Var}_{P_\theta}(\delta(X))\). The UMVUE is the decision-theoretic optimum within this restricted class.

Scoring rules: quantifying how well confidence works (Berger, Ex. 4)

A confidence rule \(C(x)\) with associated “confidence” \(\alpha(x)\): after observing \(x\), we report \(\alpha(x)\) as our confidence that \(\theta \in C(x)\). How well does \(\alpha(x)\) reflect reality?

The Brier scoring rule: \[ L_C(\theta, \alpha(x)) = (\alpha(x) - I_{C(x)}(\theta))^2. \]

When \(\theta \in C(x)\): loss = \((\alpha(x) - 1)^2\), small if \(\alpha(x) \approx 1\).

When \(\theta \notin C(x)\): loss = \(\alpha(x)^2\), small if \(\alpha(x) \approx 0\).

Scoring rules continued: constant vs. data-dependent confidence

Consider two choices: \(\alpha_1(x) \equiv 0.75\) (constant frequentist confidence) and \(\alpha_2(x) = P(\theta \in C(x) \mid x)\) (posterior probability, data-dependent confidence).

The risk of \(\alpha_1\) under the Brier loss: \[ R(\theta, \alpha_1) = E_{P_\theta}\!\left[(\alpha_1(X) - I_{C(X)}(\theta))^2\right] = 0.75^2 \cdot P_{P_\theta}(\theta \notin C(X)) + 0.25^2 \cdot P_{P_\theta}(\theta \in C(X)). \]

If \(C\) has 75% coverage: \(R(\theta, \alpha_1) = (0.5625)(0.25) + (0.0625)(0.75) = 0.1875\).

The data-dependent confidence \(\alpha_2\) adapts: it reports high confidence when the data are informative and low confidence when they are not. One can show (and we will, in Chapter 4) that \(R(\theta, \alpha_2) \leq R(\theta, \alpha_1)\) for all \(\theta\), with strict inequality for some \(\theta\).

The data-dependent confidence dominates the constant one.

Loss for prediction (Berger $$2.4.4)

Predictive problems

Predict a future observation \(Z\) with density \(g(z|\theta)\), given data \(X\) with density \(f(x|\theta)\).

If the original loss is \(L^*(z, a)\), the decision-theoretic reduction is: \[ L(\theta, a) = E_{P_\theta}[L^*(Z, a)] = \int L^*(z, a)\, g(z|\theta)\, dz. \]

This reduces the prediction problem to a standard decision problem involving \(\theta\).

Example: predicting a normal (Berger, Ex. 5)

\(Z \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta, \sigma^2)\), \(L^*(z, a) = (z - a)^2\). Then: \[ \begin{aligned} L(\theta, a) &= E_{P_\theta}[(Z - a)^2] = E_{P_\theta}[(Z - \theta)^2 + 2(Z - \theta)(\theta - a) + (\theta - a)^2] \\ &= \mathrm{Var}_{P_\theta}(Z) + 0 + (\theta - a)^2 = \sigma^2 + (\theta - a)^2. \end{aligned} \]

The cross term vanishes because \(E_{P_\theta}[Z - \theta] = 0\).

The constant \(\sigma^2\) does not affect the choice of \(a\). Choosing the best prediction of \(Z\) is equivalent to estimating \(\theta\) under squared-error loss.

This partially explains why decision theorists focus on estimating unknown parameters rather than predicting future observations: prediction problems often reduce to parameter estimation through the loss.

Vector-valued loss and criticisms

Vector-valued loss functions (Berger $$2.4.5)

It is common to evaluate procedures by several criteria simultaneously. Confidence sets are judged by (size, coverage). Sequential analysis deals with (sample size, decision loss). In medical problems one may face (“death of a patient”, “cost of a drug”) – consequences that are very hard to compare.

The practical response. Carry the components separately. If one action dominates on all components, the choice is clear. If not, perform the utility comparison only when needed. Initial subjective inputs may be crude, with refinements made only if necessary.

The theoretical reduction (Cohen and Sackrowitz, 1984). Given a vector loss \(L = (L_1, \ldots, L_J)\), treat the component index \(i\) as a parameter. Define: \[ L^*((\theta, i),\, a) = L_i(\theta, a). \] This \(L^*\) is real-valued. Risk dominance with respect to \(L^*\) (for all \(\theta\) and all \(i\)) corresponds exactly to vector risk dominance. So decision-theoretic results established for scalar losses transfer directly to vector-valued losses.

Criticisms (Berger $$2.5)

The use of loss functions is criticized on three grounds.

(i) Inappropriate for inference. Some statisticians argue that inference problems (estimation, testing, confidence sets) should not be forced into a decision-theoretic mold. We discussed this in Section 1.2 and Subsection 2.4.3. The moderate position: better inference can often be done with the aid of decision-theoretic machinery and inference losses. The decision-theoretic framework does not replace inference; it can sharpen it.

(ii) Difficult. Utility analyses can be very hard. The amount of effort depends on the importance of the problem and on the degree of refinement needed. If the conclusion is clear with only crude utility specifications, a detailed construction is unnecessary. If the loss does significantly affect the conclusion, there is no alternative to a careful utility analysis.

(iii) Nonrobust. Any loss used in practice is uncertain. Small changes in the loss function could, in principle, change the optimal action. The response: perform a sensitivity analysis. Show that reasonable variations in \(L\) do not markedly affect the conclusion. If they do, that is a signal that even more careful consideration of the loss is needed, not that the decision-theoretic approach is wrong.
